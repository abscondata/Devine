# Devine College Core Architecture

This document defines the institutional curriculum architecture for Devine College Core and records the phase logic, sequencing rationale, and current seeded state. It is intentionally severe: no elective sprawl, no decorative catalog, no fake completion.

## Program Architecture (Phased Formation)

## Constitutional Requirement Blocks (Auditable)
These blocks are the program constitution. The audit layer evaluates completion against these minimums.

- Foundations in Philosophy: minimum 1 course (3 credits). Seeded by PHIL 501, PHIL 610, PHIL 620, and PHIL 630.
- Foundations in Catholic Theology: minimum 1 course (3 credits). Seeded by THEO 510.
- Church History Core: minimum 2 courses (6 credits). Seeded by HIST 520, PATR 610, HIST 630, and HIST 640.
- Scripture Core: minimum 1 course (3 credits). Seeded by SCRP 530, SCRP 630, SCRP 640, and SCRP 650.
- Advanced Theology: minimum 3 courses (9 credits). Seeded by CONC 620, ECCL 630, LIT 640, DOGM 710, DOGM 720, DOGM 730, and DOGM 740.
- Moral Theology: minimum 2 courses (6 credits). Seeded by MORL 710 and MORL 720.
- Spiritual Theology: minimum 1 course (3 credits). Seeded by SPIR 710 and SPIR 720.
- Research and Synthesis: minimum 2 courses (6 credits). Seeded by RSYN 710 and RSYN 720.

### Phase I: Foundations (Seeded)
Purpose: Establish intellectual and doctrinal foundations that make later theological work possible and accountable.

Requirement blocks addressed:
- Foundations in Philosophy
- Foundations in Catholic Theology
- Church History Core
- Scripture Core

Approximate course count:
- 4 courses (already seeded)

Intellectual work:
- Train the philosophical act and metaphysical foundations.
- Establish revelation, faith, Scripture, and Tradition as sources of theology.
- Anchor historical consciousness in the apostolic and patristic era.
- Establish ecclesial reading of Scripture and direct biblical engagement.

Seeded courses:
- PHIL 501 - Foundations of Philosophy
- THEO 510 - Fundamental Theology
- HIST 520 - Early Church History
- SCRP 530 - Foundations of Scripture

### Phase II: Ecclesial and Conciliar Consolidation
Purpose: Consolidate doctrine and ecclesial identity through patristic, conciliar, ecclesiological, and sacramental formation. This phase connects foundations to doctrinal maturity without premature specialization.

Requirement blocks addressed:
- Advanced Theology (minimum 3 courses)
- Church History Core (minimum 2 courses)

Approximate course count:
- 4 courses

Intellectual work:
- Establish the rule of faith and early doctrinal defense in the Fathers.
- Confess conciliar doctrine with precision (Nicaea to Chalcedon).
- Clarify the nature, authority, and magisterium of the Church.
- Ground sacramental theology in Scripture, patristic witness, and magisterial definition.

Courses introduced in this phase:
- PATR 610 - Patristic Foundations: Rule of Faith and Early Doctrine
- CONC 620 - Conciliar Theology: Nicaea to Chalcedon
- ECCL 630 - Ecclesiology and Magisterium
- LIT 640 - Sacramental Theology: Baptism and Eucharist

### Phase III: Doctrinal and Moral Maturity (Begun)
Purpose: Complete advanced theology, moral theology, and spiritual formation once ecclesial foundations are secured. Advanced dogmatics begins with theological anthropology and grace; moral theology is a two-course sequence: first principles, then virtue and moral formation.

Why dogmatics starts here: after conciliar and ecclesial consolidation, theological anthropology and grace become the first mature dogmatic lane without repeating conciliar history.
Why eschatology comes next: the last things complete the dogmatic arc of creation, grace, and final consummation without widening into Marian or speculative themes.
Why Trinity and Christology synthesis follows: after conciliar history and dogmatic grounding (anthropology, grace, and eschatology), the Trinitarian and Christological center can be treated in a synthetic theological register rather than as historical definition.
Why Marian dogmatics follows: once the Christological and Trinitarian center is secure, Marian doctrine can be treated as a Christological and ecclesial locus rather than devotional ornament.
Why upper-level Scripture comes now: Scripture needed a serious advanced lane to correct institutional imbalance, and a canonical biblical-theology course deepens the whole program without duplicating foundational hermeneutics.
Why Pauline theology comes next: after canonical synthesis, Pauline theology offers depth without fragmentation, uniting grace, ecclesial identity, and resurrection hope in a disciplined scriptural arc.
Why Johannine theology follows: after canonical synthesis, Johannine theology deepens the Johannine corpus beyond the foundational John module, emphasizing Word, witness, communion, and eschatological tension without devotional drift; it is sequenced after Pauline for coherence, not as a hard dependency.
Why upper-level history comes next: the medieval formation of Latin Christendom completes the historical arc beyond the patristic period and restores institutional balance before any further specialization.
Why Reformation history follows: the medieval bridge naturally presses into the sixteenth-century rupture and Catholic reform, completing the early modern historical arc.
Why philosophical anthropology comes next: philosophy requires development beyond foundations to secure the human person, intellect, and will as stable preconditions for theology and moral formation.
Why metaphysics of nature follows: it deepens form, matter, causality, and participation as metaphysical foundations without repeating PHIL 501.
Why epistemology follows: once anthropology and metaphysics are secured, the discipline of truth, judgment, and assent can be treated explicitly to form intellectual rigor for theology, Scripture, and later synthesis.
Why moral theology starts here: after Phase II has established conciliar doctrine and ecclesial authority, the first moral course can begin with the final end, the moral act, and natural law, then the second course can complete the moral formation arc through virtue, passions, prudence, and sin.
Why spiritual theology starts here: after moral and ecclesial foundations, the spiritual life can be treated as a disciplined path of prayer, purification, and growth in charity without collapsing into moral casuistry or sacramental reduction.
Why a second spiritual course follows: once prayer and ascetical foundations are established, discernment, purification in trials, and conformity to Christ can be treated without sliding into mystical specialization or spiritual direction training.

Alternatives deferred in this phase: advanced spiritual specialization (mystical theology and direction), and any capstone-like synthesis.

Requirement blocks addressed:
- Advanced Theology (completed here)
- Moral Theology
- Spiritual Theology
- Scripture Core (deepened beyond minimum)
- Church History Core (deepened beyond minimum)
- Foundations in Philosophy (deepened beyond minimum)

Approximate course count:
- 16 to 18 courses

Intellectual work:
- Mature dogmatic synthesis (anthropology, grace, eschatology).
- Trinitarian and Christological synthesis beyond conciliar history.
- Marian dogmatics grounded in Scripture, Christology, and ecclesiology.
- Canonical biblical theology that unifies covenant, kingdom, temple, and new creation.
- Pauline theology that integrates grace, ecclesial identity, and resurrection hope.
- Johannine theology that deepens Word, witness, communion, and eschatological tension.
- Medieval Church history that bridges patristic foundations to later reform pressures.
- Reformation and Catholic Reform completing the early modern historical arc.
- Philosophical anthropology grounding person, intellect, will, and freedom for later theology and moral formation.
- Metaphysics of nature deepening substance, causality, teleology, and participation.
- Epistemology of truth, judgment, and assent as intellectual discipline.
- Moral theology rooted in beatitude, moral act, and law, then extending to virtue and moral formation.
- Spiritual theology grounded in discernment, purification, and conformity to Christ.
- Sacramental theology beyond initiation (penance, orders, marriage) if warranted.

### Phase IV: Research and Synthesis (Capstone)
Purpose: Demonstrate integrated mastery and rigorous synthesis rather than isolated content accumulation. RSYN 710 forms method; RSYN 720 delivers a full thesis (7000-10000 words) with an 800-1200 word defense memo.

Requirement blocks addressed:
- Research and Synthesis

Approximate course count:
- 2 courses

End-state expectation:
- A method and thesis-architecture bridge course followed by a 7000-10000 word capstone thesis with an 800-1200 word defense memo.
- Explicit integration of philosophy, Scripture, doctrinal theology, history, and ecclesiology.
- Oral defense or structured critique sequence based on stored critiques and revisions.

## Prerequisite Logic Across Phases
- Phase I establishes all baseline prerequisites.
- Phase II requires foundations before ecclesial and conciliar consolidation.
- Phase III depends on Phase II doctrinal consolidation.
- Phase IV depends on completion of Phase III doctrinal and moral maturity.

## What Is Intentionally Deferred
- Advanced moral theology (bioethics, social doctrine) until doctrinal and ecclesial foundations are secured.
- Specialized dogmatic treatises (other specialized dogmatic loci) until later Phase III.
- Specialized Scripture lanes (other canonical specializations) until Johannine depth is absorbed.
- Canon law and disciplinary studies until after ecclesiology and sacramental theology.
  - Epistemology and philosophy of knowledge (now seeded).
  - Advanced spiritual theology (mystical theology, spiritual direction, particular schools) until after foundational spiritual formation.
- Publication layer or public writing features (not part of this institution phase).

## Rationale and Guardrails
- The sequence is cumulative: each phase gives necessary conditions for the next.
- Councils and creeds are not treated as isolated data; they are integrated with patristic sources and ecclesial authority.
- No course should duplicate another by topic or outcome. Distinction and progression are required.
- No premature specialization: a course is seeded only if its prerequisites and institutional role are defensible.
- Completion logic remains strict: only completed readings count toward progress.

## Cautions Against Drift
- Do not add electives or attractive topics without phase rationale.
- Do not collapse multiple disciplines into a single course to save time.
- Do not add courses that the prerequisite chain does not support.
- Do not treat institutional sequence as a catalog; it is a formation path.
